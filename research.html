I am interested in structure, language and inference.

How are things put together? Can we capture a given phenomenon precisely and faithfully? Can we find a way to make reasoning about a particular domain natural and convenient?

This manifests in the study of category theory and type theory. Category theory is the mathematics of structure (sometimes called the mathematics of mathematics—because mathematics is all about structure anyway, right?): it studies classes of objects and transformations between them. Type theories are formal languages in which we can write proofs (as in mathematics or logic) and programs (as in computing); actually, in type theory these are the same thing.

Categorical semantics studies models of type theory: categories (structures) supporting a notion of inference. Models can be used to establish properties of type theories (eg. consistency). Conversely, type theories provide domain-specific “internal languages” for reasoning about/inside categories. Well-designed type theories can indeed make reasoning about a particular domain a lot simpler than by some other method.

Much of my work has focused on realizability models of type theory. Realizability reveals computational content of logical and mathematical theories. For my PhD, I developed higher-dimensional realizability models of intensional type theory based on groupoids, wherein realizers themselves carry higher-dimensional structure.
