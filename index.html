I am interested in structure, language and inference.

How are things put together? Can we describe a particular phenomenon faithfully and precisely? Can we find a way to make reasoning about a particular domain natural and convenient?

This manifests in the study of category theory and type theory. Category theory is the mathematics of structure: it studies classes of objects and transformations between them. Type theories are formal languages in which proofs and programs (these are two sides of the same coin) live as first-class objects.

Categorical semantics tells us what kind of structure is required to model a given type theory. Type theories provide domain-specific “internal languages” for reasoning about/inside particular categories. Well-designed type theories can indeed make reasoning about a particular domain a lot simpler than by some other method.

The flavour of categorical model of type theory that much of my work has focused on is realizability models. Realizability gives us insight into the computational content of logical and mathematical theories. In my PhD thesis I developed higher-dimensional realizability models of intensional type theory based on groupoids, wherein realizers themselves carry higher dimensional structure.
